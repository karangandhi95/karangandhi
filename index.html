TrainingBuddy

TrainingBuddy is an application created to help inexperienced gymgoers become familiar with the gym. The application offers the user a wide variety of different workout schedules, as well as an explanation on how to perform a particular exercise.

Tools

The tools used in this development cycle ranged from research methods, wireframing tools and prototyping tools used for creating both Lo-Fi and Hi-Fi prototypes.
- Balsamiq for wireframing
- Figma for Lo-Fi and Hi-fi prototyping

Approach

The focus with this prototype was to create a highly usable application that would aid newcomers in the gym with their workout. Our primary focus with the prototype was for it to have a high degree of usefulness and fluency, meaning that the user would get the hang of the usability of the prototype very quickly. This was achieved firstly through semi-structured interviews where we asked questions relating to the users goals, needs and encountered problems in the gym. The user group consisted of men and women who were beginners in the gym. The users were chosen by being asked "do you go the gym, and if so, do you know how to structure your workout in the gym?" Those who responded by saying that they were unfamiliar with the gym were chosen to be in the user group. In total we had 15 people in the usergroup.

The user test consisted of the users interacting with a Lo-Fi interactive prototype created through sigma. Even though Figma is a Hi-Fi tool the prototype used in the test was quite "empty", meaning it was more similar to wireframes. Figma was used for the interactive capabilites. The users were asked to navigate to certain pages within the prototype and complete certain tasks. The difficulty of finding the pages and completing the tasks were then used to assess the usability of the prototype. The users were also asked how we could improve upon the application.

Final prototype

The needs of the users were many; a training guide, a motivational trainer, logbook, comparing results to friends, etc. The constraints meant that we would not be able to prototype everything the user wanted so the primary focus was on the actual training guide.

SmartShield

Ideation

SmartShield was a prototype ideated within a course in HCI in Stockholm University. The goal was for the project group to create a hi-fi prototype with interactive capabilities. Most groups decided to stay within the sphere of applications and websites whereas our group wanted to deviate from the predicted path. We decided to brainstorm and see what we came up with. We came up with SmartShield through our final converging session where we improved upon ideas and chose one.

SmartShield

SmartShield is a 3D simulated prototype that uses Augmented Reality to project digital screens on to the cars windshield. The idea for SmartShield came about through a casual discussion regarding road safety. A recent study had shown that many of the road accidents were caused by smartphone usage during driving. We wanted to create something that would help reduce the number of accidents and make the road safer for all drivers. As this was a prototype highly dependent on the userbase we wanted to spend a considerable amount of time on the actual user testing.

User Research

The user research consisted of the use of several research tools; questionnaire, semi-formal interviews, lo-fi prototype, hi-fi prototype and the "talk-aloud" method. The users were chosen through a survey which confirmed their experience with driving as well as their age. We were looking for users with a high level of driving experience within the age of 20-65 as this is the age group with the highest working cognitive function. The first user test was conducted with a Lo-Fi prototype using the talk-aloud method. Before the test started the user had to fill in a questionnaire, and at the end we conducted a small semi-formal interview. All of the user tests were video recorded.

Lo-Fi prototype

The Lo-Fi prototype was created by using a sheet of A3 paper and pen and paper. The idea here was to create a paper windshield with compononents that the user could then move around in order to customize the interface to their liking. The components we chose to include in the artifact were; music, navigation, speed, fuel and phone notifications. A picture would be taken of all the "windshields" at the end. We then put together these pictures into heatmaps which showed us were the users preferred to put the different functions.

Photos taken during the Lo-Fi user tests. On the left users are interacting with the prototype. Shown on the right are the generated heatmaps indicating the preferred placements of the different functions.

The Lo-Fi user test was concluded with a semi-formal interview regarding their driving in traffic.
